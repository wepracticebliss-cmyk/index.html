The Foundation

What Are Executive
Function Skills?

Executive functions are the cognitive control systems that govern how we make decisions, manage time, regulate emotions, and follow through on goals. Think of them as the brain's command center — housed in the prefrontal cortex and shaping nearly every aspect of daily life.

While experts don't always agree on exact numbers or names, there is consensus: executive functions govern how we manage work, relationships, and long-term goals. They can be strengthened at any age — and coaching is one of the most effective ways to do it.

"Just as we teach history and geography, we need to teach children how their brains and bodies work."

— Bessel van der Kolk, The Body Keeps the Score

Does any of this sound familiar?

●Struggling to start tasks even when you know they're important
●Finishing projects in a last-minute rush after weeks of avoidance
●Multiple to-do lists — and still feeling more overwhelmed
●Knowing how to get organized but unable to maintain those systems
●Consistently running late or losing track of time
●Emotional reactions that feel larger than the situation warrants

You're not lazy or broken. Your executive function skills may simply need support, structure, and the right framework — and that's exactly what coaching provides.

The 12 Executive
Function Skills

Understanding your own EF profile — where you thrive and where you struggle — is the first step toward lasting change.

01
Response Inhibition

Thinking before acting — evaluating a situation and how your behavior might affect it before responding.

02
Working Memory

Holding and using information in the moment — essential for reasoning, decisions, and conversation.

03
Emotional Control

Managing emotions to complete tasks and achieve goals — responding thoughtfully, not reactively.

04
Task Initiation

Beginning projects without excessive delay — even when the task feels daunting or unclear.

05
Sustained Attention

Maintaining focus despite distractibility, fatigue, or boredom — the engine behind follow-through.

06
Planning & Prioritizing

Creating a roadmap to a goal and deciding what truly matters versus what can wait.

07
Organization

Building and maintaining systems to track information and materials — physically and mentally.

08
Time Management

Estimating and allocating time — with a felt sense that time is finite and worth protecting.

09
Cognitive Flexibility

Adapting when plans change — revising course in response to obstacles, mistakes, or new information.

10
Metacognition

Stepping back to observe your own thinking — asking "How am I doing?" and adjusting accordingly.

11
Goal-Directed Persistence

Holding a goal through to completion — not derailed by competing interests or discouragement.

12
Stress Tolerance

Thriving under pressure — coping with uncertainty, change, and performance demands without overwhelm.

How We Grow

How Executive Functions Develop

We are not born with executive function skills — but the potential is always there. These skills develop through growth, experience, and the environments we inhabit. Understanding this helps us approach ourselves and our children with greater compassion.

For Children

By age 3, children are already developing executive functions — responding to stimuli, following rules, managing impulses. As they grow, they learn to plan, set goals, and initiate tasks. EF skills are typically fully developed between ages 18–25.

  • Extreme stress can limit development
  • Exposure to environmental toxins
  • Chronic poverty and adversity
  • Traumatic brain injuries
  • Neurological differences (ADHD, autism)

For Adults

While development peaks before 25, adults absolutely can build and refine executive function skills. Coaching, repeated practice, and a supportive environment make this not only possible — but genuinely transformative.

Understanding ADHD

ADHD & Executive Function

ADHD is one of the most discussed — and misunderstood — conditions in modern life. A both/and approach honours the biological realities of ADHD while recognizing the profound impact of our emotional environments.

The Science

Neurobiology & Genetics

Twin studies consistently show ADHD heritability at 70–80%. Brain imaging reveals structural differences in regions governing attention and impulse control — particularly the prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia. Environmental factors influence severity, but neurobiology is central. Stimulant medications are well-supported by evidence for reducing core ADHD symptoms across all ages.

The Human Dimension

Gabor Maté's Perspective

Dr. Gabor Maté proposes that ADHD arises partly from early disruptions in attachment — when a child's emotional needs aren't adequately met, the developing brain adapts by tuning out as a defense. Healing, in his view, comes through emotional connection and relational repair. This lens invites us to look beyond labels and see the whole person behind the diagnosis.

Both & And

The most helpful stance honours the biological realities of ADHD and recognizes the profound impact of emotional environments. Very little about the human mind is ever black and white. This practice is also hormone-informed — recognizing that hormonal cycles profoundly shape attention, mood, and cognition, particularly for women navigating ADHD across the lifespan.

The Psychology of Flow

Flow — the state of complete absorption in a meaningful challenge — is both the goal of great coaching and its most powerful reward. When executive function skills are strengthened, flow becomes accessible.

"Because attention determines what will or will not appear in consciousness, and because it is also required to make any other mental events, it is useful to think of it as psychic energy. We create ourselves by how we invest this energy. Memories, thoughts, and feelings are all shaped by how we use it. And it is an energy under our control, hence, attention is our most important tool in the task of improving the quality of experience."

— Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, Flow

"Enjoyment appears at the boundary between boredom and anxiety, when the challenges are just balanced with the person's capacity to act."

— Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, Flow

The 8 Components of Enjoyment

1 A completable challenge — tasks we have a realistic chance of finishing
2 Concentration — the ability to fully focus on what we are doing
3 Clear goals — knowing exactly what we are working toward
4 Immediate feedback — a felt sense of progress as we go
5 Effortless involvement — worries and frustrations fade from awareness
6 Sense of control — feeling agency over one's actions
7 Loss of self-consciousness — yet a stronger sense of self emerges afterward
8 Altered time perception — hours pass like minutes, or minutes stretch like hours
